Matrix Recursions: Spectral vs Numerical Radius

The recursion \(y_t = A y_{t-1}\)

  • Closed form: [ y_t = A^t,y_0. ]

  • Two questions drive the dynamics:

    1. Does \(A^t \to 0\) (stability) or does it grow (instability)?
    2. Can \(\lVert A^t\rVert\) exhibit transient amplification even if the system is asymptotically stable?

  • The answers depend on how we measure “size” and on whether \(A\) is normal (commutes with \(A^*\)) or non-normal.

Spectral radius: the asymptotic growth rate

  • Definition (spectral radius): [ (A) := { || : (A)}. ]

  • Stability (discrete time): [ (A) < 1 A^t  (t). ]

  • Intuition: eigenvalues give the long-run scaling of invariant “modes” (exactly for diagonalizable/normal matrices; asymptotically for all).

  • But: eigenvalues alone can miss large short-run growth when \(A\) is non-normal.

Intuition - \(w(A)\) captures the largest possible “one-step gain” of the scalar quadratic form \(x^*Ax\). - For normal matrices: \(\rho(A)=w(A)=\lVert A\rVert_2\). - For non-normal matrices: often \(\rho(A) \ll w(A)\).

Why \(w(A)\) matters for dynamics (transient growth)

Even when \(\rho(A)<1\) (asymptotically stable), a non-normal matrix can have:

  • large \(\lVert A\rVert_2\) (big one-step stretching)
  • large \(w(A)\) (big one-step Rayleigh-quotient magnitude)
  • transient amplification: \(\lVert A^t\rVert\) can increase for some \(t\) before eventually decaying.
A canonical 2×2 example: [ A = \[\begin{bmatrix}0.9 & M\\ 0 & 0.9\end{bmatrix}\]

,M. ]

  • Eigenvalues are both \(0.9\) so \(\rho(A)=0.9<1\).
  • But for large \(M\), \(\lVert A\rVert_2\) (and typically \(w(A)\)) can be much larger than 1, producing sizeable short-run growth.

Spectral vs numerical radius: the punchline

  • Spectral radius \(\rho(A)\)
    • Governs asymptotic stability and long-run exponential rate.
    • Insensitive to how non-orthogonal the eigenvectors are.
  • Numerical radius \(w(A)\)
    • Upper-bounds \(\rho(A)\) and is closely tied to the geometry of the field of values.
    • More informative about one-step amplification and non-normality.
  • Rule of thumb
    • If \(A\) is (nearly) normal: \(\rho(A)\) tells most of the story.
    • If \(A\) is non-normal: expect transients; look at \(w(A)\), \(\lVert A\rVert_2\), and (if needed) pseudospectra.

Intuition - Rotating \(A\) by a phase \(e^{-i\theta}\) rotates the field of values. - Taking the real part selects the supporting line (tangent) of the rotated set. - Maximizing the support function over \(\theta\) recovers the farthest distance from the origin.

Connecting the two: radii and long-run behavior

  • If you care about eventual decay/growth of \(y_t=A^t y_0\):
    • the decisive quantity is \(\rho(A)\) (via eigenvalues, or via Gelfand’s formula).
  • If you care about short-run amplification, robustness, or sensitivity:
    • examine \(w(A)\) and \(\lVert A\rVert_2\) (and, for deeper analysis, pseudospectra).
  • In many economic/DSGE linearizations:
    • \(\rho(A)<1\) corresponds to stable laws of motion.
    • large \(w(A)\) / \(\lVert A\rVert_2\) can still mean substantial transient responses (large multipliers) even under stability.
